What is MAS?
McCune-Albright Syndrome (MAS) is a complex genetic disorder that affects the bone, skin, and endocrine systems. It is characterized by hyperpigmented skin lesions known as 'cafe-au-lait' spots, hyperfunctioning endocrine disease, and fibrous dysplasia. MAS is caused by a sporadic, early postzygotic somatic mutation in the GNAS1 gene. While gene mutations in comic books or movies often result in superheroes like Spider-Man or the Hulk, in reality, the consequences of gene mutations on humans are severe. They can lead to organ dysfunction, severe DNA damage, and weakened immune systems. In fact, the majority of genetic diseases, including cancer, are primarily caused by gene mutations.
MAS is classified as a rare disease, estimated to affect 1 in 100,000 to 1 in 1,000,000 individuals in the general population. This is approximately the same likelihood as winning second or third prize in the lottery. However, with over 7000 rare diseases, the total number of rare disease patients worldwide is approximately 475 million, with children accounting for two-thirds of the cases. Due to the small patient population for each rare disease and limited research funding from pharmaceutical companies, there are still thousands of rare diseases without approved specific treatment medications. Currently, 95% of rare disease patients, including those with MAS, lack effective treatments.
Caring for MAS patients requires both time and financial resources. Some schools may hesitate to enroll MAS children due to concerns about potential fractures. Unfortunately, MAS is not detectable during routine pregnancy tests, so parents with concerns will have to seek advanced pregnancy testing methods.
